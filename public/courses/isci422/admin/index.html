Objectives

There are four primary goals for the course. The student should be able to...

  1. Define science and explain.
  2. Define scientific model and explain.
  3. Deconstruct a scientific model.
  4. Construct a scientific model.

To achieve these goals the course will be divided into three sections.

Section 1: Definition

Week 0: Sep 9 - Sep 11

Lecture
Seminar
Science in context
    110 min remaining.
    1. Prep: Print out course syllabus.
    2. Prep: find a topic of debate with supporting papers for each side and make copies for every student. One position should be unscientific. See http://www.actionbioscience.org/evolution/nhmag.html for example.
    3. (30 min) Distribute and discuss syllabus.
    4. (30 min) Discuss the scientific method.
    5. (45 min) Expand discussion to definition of science. What is not science? (Other 'ways of knowing'.)
    6. (5 min) Homework: Read debate papers before next class. Focus: Which arguments are scientific and which aren't? Why?

    Lecture
    What is science?    		Seminar
    Definition of science
    1. Prep: Print and bring Assignment 1 instructions (1 per group).
    2. (10 min) Assign groups for class discussion & Assignment 1.
    3. (30 min) Groups: discuss scientific merits of arguments & definition of science.
    4. (30 min) Class: discuss same.
    5. (30 min) Discuss logic of science. Transposition: (If Theory then Evidence supports) == (If not Evidence supports then not Theory). Goal is to have students discover the concept of falsifiability.
    6. (10 min) Homework: Assign groups for Assignment 1 (how would you test evolution vs. ID?).
    1. Prep: Print and bring handouts for Project 1.
    2. Prep: Bring signup sheet for Assignment 2.
    3. (30 min) Have groups construct definitions of science on board.
    4. (30 min) Discuss definitions of science. What's common? What's different? What's missing? What's superfluous?
    5. (30 min) Contrast theories that are scientific but have been proven false versus unscientific theories. Eg. plum-pudding model of atom versus "Rik's(?) pet monster". A theory that's wrong may still be scientific.
    6. (10 min) Assignment 2: distribute signup sheet. Students must bring typical walk & run speeds for their animal next lecture.
    7. (10 min) Project 1: Distribute handouts and explain.

    Week 2: Sep 21 - Sep 25

    Lecture
    Project 2: Brainstorming    		Seminar
    Our first model
    1. Prep: make handouts for Project 2.
    2. (15 min) Project 2: Distribute handouts and explain.
    3. (5 min) Form groups of 3. Explain plan: to brainstorm a research question.
    4. (30 min) Storm: Each member takes 10 minutes to come up with as many ideas for investigation as possible. No criticism or justification. Just a long list (at least 10).
    5. (10 min) Reflection: Individually review possibilities, eliminate all but three favourites.
    6. (30 min) Discussion: New groups of 3. Each member explores favourite ideas and gets suggestions from others. Are they scientific?
    7. (20 min) Wrap up?
    1. Project 1: Article Selection due.
    2. Prep: bring projector & laptop w/ spreadsheet for Assignment 2.
    3. Prep: Print Haldane's 'On Being the Right Size' for every student.
    4. (50 min) Develop model of walking. Invoke theories of evolution (energy minimization) & Newton's laws (pendulum period).
    5. (30 min) Fill in spreadsheet from data provided by students (give students credit for Assignment 2).
    6. (20 min) Discuss chipmunk model. How good was it? How could it be improved? What else could account for relationship?
    7. (10 min) Assignment 3: Nitpick a movie using allometric scaling arguments.

    Week 3: Sep 28 - Oct 2

    Lecture
    Models in science    		Seminar
    Taxonomy of models
    1. (50 min) Discuss models in general. What are they for? How do they relate to theory and experiments?
    2. (30 min) Discuss stepping-stone model of scientific models. Theory <--> Model <--> Experiment, related through hypotheses and tests.
    3. (30 min) Groups: Come up with concrete examples of these elements.
    1. Project 1: Grouping due.
    2. Project 2: Inspiration due.
    3. (20 min) Discuss: how should we classify models? By what criterion? Why? How does classification aid us? (Hint: don't segregate by disciplines.)
    4. (30 min) Form groups. Come up with a taxonomy of models.
    5. (30 min) Discuss: which group has the most useful taxonomy? Why? (Vote: Points for best?)
    6. (10 min) Explain Project 2 lit search assignment. Due next seminar.
    20 min remaining.

    Section 2: Deconstruction

    Week 4: Oct 5 - Oct 9

    Lecture
    Analytic modeling I    		Seminar
    Model definition
    1. (10 min) How do analytic models (specifically: Reaction Kinetics) relate to other models? (Taxonomy: Quantitative -> Mathematical -> Analytic)
    2. (10 min) Match up strong and weak math students.
    3. (20 min) Review reaction kinetics (eg. A + 2B --> 3B + 4C).
    4. (20 min) Analytical methods: nullclines & flows.
    5. (40 min) Go through as many examples as possible: Birth, Death, Birth+Death, Competition, Predation.
    6. (10 min) Assignment (Analytic homework): As partners, extend the last model in some way. Get instructor approval before the end of class. Solve for nullclines and draw flows. Discuss the strengths and weaknesses of analytical modeling. Due next lecture. Partners may work together but must submit their own work.
    1. Project 1: Definition due.
    2. (20 min) Explain Project 1 Stage 2.
    3. (10 min) Project 1 groups all write their definition of scientific model on the blackboard.
    4. (10 min) Each group reads out their definition.
    5. (30 min) Discuss definition of scientific model. What are minimal/essential ingredients?
    6. (20 min) Each group discusses and revises their definition on the board.
    7. (20 min) Each group appends a hand-written definition and justification (one page) to their definition report before submitting it. New definition and rationale will be evaluated as part of report.
