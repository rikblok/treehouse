Objective

Your first project serves two objectives: to begin you will examine published examples of scientific work that utilize models in order to develop a definition of the term "model" as used in science. Then, using the results of your research, you will discover that it is possible to construct different models of the same phenomenon and that the different models allow one to approach a research question in different ways.

Stages

This project represents a substantial part of the course and your final marks. To keep you on track the project will be broken down into a number of stages:

Model Definition

Working in groups of four (assigned), search the scientific literature to find two papers that use a model as a central part of the work. Each paper must have been published recently1 in a major journal2. Each group will be assigned topic areas within which to work. Find one article in each area. You may go to the library to research but each group must return to the seminar room to have their choice of papers approved3 before the end of class.

Working in groups write a short report (see the template for page limits and formatting requirements) that

  • For each article:
    • Briefly explains what phenomenon is being studied;
    • Justifies why the researchers chose this model and/or type of model;
  • Discusses themes common to both papers, especially their use of models; and
  • Concludes with a brief (25 words or less) definition of "scientific model".

You should not try to explain the models, just rationalize their use. Append a hard copy of the source papers to the report.

Included in the report must be an assessment of each group member's contribution to the entire project effort. The distribution of credit must be agreed upon by the group and sum to 60.

Model Deconstruction

Choose one of your assigned topic areas to investigate further. Search recent scientific literature1,2 to find papers3 that employ models to investigate a phenomenon or system that interests your group. From these papers select two that use very different types of model of the same system or phenomenon. Take care that you do not select multiple examples of the same type of model. The types of model must differ in:

  • Level of abstraction,
  • Means of implementation (numerical, analytical, statistical, simulation, scale, representative organism, etc.), or
  • Some other way (approved by us).

Write a report, including illustrations and references (see the template for page limits and formatting requirements) that:

  1. Briefly describes the phenomenon or system being modeled,
  2. Describes, explains and contrasts the models you have chosen,
  3. Outlines (hidden) assumptions in each model, and
  4. Explains and contrasts the sorts of research questions that are addressed by the different models.
The submitted report should include hard copies of your source articles (but do not assume we will read them--your report should contain all the information required to understand their significance).

Included in the report must be an assessment of each group member's contribution to the entire project effort. The distribution of credit must be agreed upon by the group and sum to 60.

Notes

1  This depends on the field but in most of the sciences today 10 years is recent.

2  This is very difficult to define from outside a field. Usually major journals are published by big publishing houses (Kluwer, Elsevier, Pergamon, etc., in the sciences) or by national professional societies or associations in major countries. We want you to think about this but know that the only thing that really counts is experience in a field.

3  During your research for the definition stage you will find many examples of models in your assigned areas. Do not discard these articles--they will be useful for the deconstruction stage of the project. You may re-use an article you reported on in the definition stage or others you had considered.
